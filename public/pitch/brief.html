The state of agentic AI

Capability is now table stakes. The operating model is the moat.

Agentic AI is no longer a research curiosity — it's a procurement category. But the data is sobering. The agentic initiatives funded in 2025 are being cancelled at a rate that suggests the bottleneck has moved.

Over 40% of agentic AI projects are projected to be cancelled by end-2027 — cited reasons: escalating costs, unclear business value, inadequate risk controls.

Gartner press release, June 25, 2025.

None of those reasons are model failures. They are operating-model failures — the gap between what a system can do in a demo and what it survives in production. The companies that make it through the cancellation wave will be the ones who treated agentic AI as an engineering and governance discipline from the first commit.

How we work

Two scores per engagement. One risk equation underneath.

CSI — Complexity Score Index

A 0–100 read on the engineering surface area an agentic system actually carries: context load, agent handoffs, tool count, and how much is delegated to the model.

What it estimates: the probability of failure under real-world load.

SPS — Security Posture Score

A 0–100 rubric covering data separation, capability segregation, action gates, and append-only auditability. Anchored in Simon Willison's Lethal Trifecta framework.

What it contains: the blast radius when failure happens.

The math underneath

Expected loss = Probability of failure × Blast radius

CSI estimates probability. SPS contains blast radius. Every engagement reduces both, and the report discloses its scoring weights and inputs — no proprietary black boxes. The reader can fill in their own numbers and check the work.

Human-in-the-loop, by design

HITL isn't a setting. It's the seam between what agents do and what humans decide.

We design every system around an explicit boundary between autonomous action and human decision. Three reasons it matters — none of them about values, all of them about risk economics.

01

HITL is the audit boundary.

Every agentic decision either has a human checkpoint or it doesn't — and that boundary is what auditors, regulators, and insurers will ask about. Without HITL, an audit log is recording autonomous decisions nobody approved. With HITL, the log is evidence. The seam is what makes the log meaningful.

02

HITL is the reversibility budget.

Agents that can't be interrupted or corrected are liabilities, not assets. We design systems for the cost of being wrong, not just the upside of being right. The cheaper the rollback path, the higher the autonomy we can responsibly allow. That's not a moral position — it's hedging.

03

HITL is the enterprise trust unlock.

The companies that scale agentic AI inside regulated environments are the ones who built governance in from week one. HITL isn't friction — it's the precondition for getting near a regulated workflow at all. As cyber and AI liability insurance markets mature, demonstrable human-approval controls are becoming underwriting requirements.

HITL isn't slower. It's the cheapest insurance premium you'll ever pay against an agent decision you can't defend.

What AI-native actually means

A different operating model, not a different feature stack.

There's a real difference between AI features bolted onto a legacy operation and a company built around agents from day one. Three observations from the leading AI-native organizations of 2025 — each one a deliberate operating choice, not a feature flag.

01

Smaller surface, larger leverage.

The companies on the Forbes AI 50 (2025) operate at fundamentally smaller headcount-to-revenue ratios than their legacy peers. AI-native isn't a tech stack — it's an operating model where one person can carry the work of a team, because the agents carry the rest.

02

Pilots aren't products.

Research from MIT's State of AI in Business 2025 reports that roughly 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots fail to produce measurable P&L impact. The gap isn't model quality — it's the absence of evals, governance, and operating discipline. The same gap CSI and SPS are designed to close.

03

Pricing follows outcomes, not seats.

Analysis from a16z and Sequoia points to the same shift: AI-native businesses charge per outcome — resolved tickets, completed transactions, units of generated work — not per seat. Seat-based SaaS economics don't survive a system that does the work of a team.

AI-native is a discipline. It's measurable from the outside if you know what to look for — which is exactly what an assessment does.
